Quick Snapshot: Where the Project Stands at the End of Sprint #4

What we’re doing: Our team is creating a website that is essentially a clone of Eventbrite. The site allows users to create new events and post them to the site. It also allows users to view upcoming events and RSVP to those that interest them, provided they have a Yale e-mail address.
The team had a really productive sprint this week. Of the 39 tests we run for the site, only 2 are not yet passing! Here is a link to the home page. You’ll notice that:
- Upcoming events appear on the homepage, and past events do not.
- Each event links to an event detail page that includes pertinent information about the event.
- The event detail page also allows a user to RSVP for the event, provided the user has a Yale e-mail address.
- All non-Yale e-mail addresses produce an error message when they try to RSVP:

- You probably won’t notice this, but when you visit the page /api/events you’ll retrieve an object with key events containing all events in JSON format.

You’ll also notice some things that aren’t quite up to snuff yet:
- With the exception of the “About” page, the format of the site is pretty basic. This was a strategic decision on the part of the team, as we wanted to get the functionality of the site down before we expand on its visual appeal.

Recap of the week’s sprint

Scrum meetings:
- Tuesday, November 10
- Wednesday, November 11
- Monday, November 16
The team met several times this week, which helped our productivity. Our Tuesday meeting was our longest of the week, and we spent the bulk of the time reviewing the previous week’s sprint. We decided to include all of our comments and notes on progress for our user stories on Waffle.io. We had previously been using a Google Document to communicate our overall progress. We committed to evening out the work so that each person was devoting 2 to 6 hours of time to the sprint. We also reaffirmed our commitment to building the functionality of the site--its basic architecture--before working on the style and user experience aspects. We had less time to go over our plans for the upcoming sprint, but we were able to dole out individual assignments. As a result, our site now passes almost all of the required tests. Our communication was much better, and every member of the team completed a user story during the sprint. For the past week, Lea was the scrum master and Jeff was the product owner.
We treated the subsequent meetings as stand-up meetings, and we were able to clarify our assignments and troubleshoot any issues we were having.
The biggest challenge we’re facing is finding the time for two things:
1. To express our expectations for ourselves, each other, and the group. There’s still some conflicts about how much we should be doing each week and our overall goals for the project. Our next scrum meeting will take some time to give everyone a chance to chime in about these topics.
2. Creating new, slightly more detailed user stories with regards to the new requirements and our vision for the site’s visual style and user experience.
We’ve put these things at the top of our agenda for our next big scrum meeting. Still, we’ve made major progress, and we’re really happy with how things are going.
