Estimate the story points

 

With the exception of class assignments, no team member has significant programming experience and therefore it was difficult to estimate durations of the stories we planned to complete for Sprint-1.  Instead, we used a combination of a relative size vs. time estimate and planning poker (without the cards) to approximate story completion times.  

 

Similar to Sprint-1, each team member was asked to privately rank stories they felt the team would be able to complete in the coming week.  We then compared everyone’s list to see which stories were considered doable by all members and which were not.  Every story that was considered doable for the week was added to our sprint list and the rest were discussed to understand why they were not doable. Through this conversation we realized that these tasks were achievable (we realized some team members had a better grasp of the story requirements) and so were added to Sprint-2 story log.  

 

For stories that were not included on anyone’s list, we decided we were going to check progress during our next sprint meeting.  We believed our first sprint would give everyone a better understanding of how long each story would take.  



 

Overall Review of Sprint 2

 

While it is generally not advisable to conduct sprint review meetings during actual sprint meetings but because team members were so busy we had no choice but to consolidate the two meetings into one.  During the review of Sprint-1 story list we realized we only completed 3-4 stories which was significantly less than the originally planned 15 stories that were allocated for Sprint-1.  This was due to technical issues we encountered with the npm install command and reconfiguring our workflow process.  We also underestimated the amount of time it would take us to set up a github-cloud9-heroku workflow process.  For example, we originally only set up one workspace on Cloud9 which meant everyone was working on the same Cloud9 files.  While this allowed us to work simultaneously on the same file, we felt this did not accurately simulate a realistic agile workflow experience.  We then collectively researched how to create different workspaces, push and pull from the github repository, and to create branches.   The only exception to establishing an authentic agile workflow is that each change that is pushed to Github is automatically added to the master repository.  We realize in most agile teams, a project leader will most likely review before committing (along with team) before committing to the repository.

 

We then went through the remaining user stories and distributed tasks based on current comfort level with coding of individual team members. While the work ahead this week is ambitious we decided to add several stories to make up for the slower productivity of week 1.  

 

Future Plans

For next sprint, we will start adding actual functions to the project. Our plan is to complete the basic format of each page. Then, we will work on homepage and 'About Us' page. We are planning to hold another scrum meeting at the end of this week to analyze our progress and performance. During this meeting, we also plan to discuss the remaining backlog, tests, and what should be accepted as a complete story.
